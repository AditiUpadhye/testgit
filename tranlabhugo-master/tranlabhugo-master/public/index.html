What We Study

Our lab at the Indiana University School of Medicine uses systems-based approaches to investigate the host response to P. falciparum infection and malaria vaccines as it relates to host tolerance and malaria protection. Thus, the major questions driving our research are:
1. How do malaria-exposed individuals learn to tolerate Plasmodium infections, sometimes at parasite densities in the hundreds of thousands of parasites per microliter?
2. Which immune responses induced by either naturally acquired malaria or candidate malaria vaccines are associated with protection from malaria infection and disease?

Defining clinical and sterile immunity to P. falciparum infection using systems biology approaches

Funding organization: National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases (K08)

Each year malaria afflicts ~200 million people and causes over 430,000 deaths, primarily among African infants. Although a first-generation malaria vaccine is now available, it is only modestly effective in the target population of African infants; thus, a better understanding of natural immunity to Plasmodium falciparum, the deadliest of malaria parasites, in young African children can inform efforts to develop the next generation of malaria vaccines. Using samples collected from young Malian children before and during natural P. falciparum infections, this study aims to identify predictors and mechanisms of malaria immunity that aid the development of future malaria vaccines.

Investigating the effects of Plasmodium falciparum on the epigenetic landscape of human immune cells

Funding organization: Ralph W. and Grace M. Showalter Research Trust

Understanding how silent P. falciparum infections are established and how they affect the host immune system could lead to new strategies to identify and control malaria. Other infectious microbes have been shown to affect the host immune system by modifying the host epigenome, which are chemical marks on the genome that can affect gene activity without changing the underlying genetic code. We hypothesize that P. falciparum interacts with the human epigenome to blunt the normally vigorous inflammatory response to infection, allowing the parasite to persist. We are studying the effects of P. falciparum on the epigenome of human cells using a cell culture model of P. falciparum infection and blood cells from African children with silent P. falciparum infections. Insights from this study may help guide the development of treatments that reduce the severity of malaria infections.

Systems analysis to understand protective immunity induced by an attenuated sporozoite malaria vaccine in African infants

Funding organization: Doris Duke Charitable Foundation

We are using systems biology approaches to determine the molecular and cellular correlates of protection from natural P. falciparum infection in a clinical trial of the PfSPZ Vaccine. We aim to 1) determine blood transcriptional signatures before and after immunization that predict sterile protection or a delay to parasitemia; 2) determine molecular correlates of durable PfSPZ immunogenicity; and 3) perform integrative analysis of all measurements from molecular and immunological assays to determine the variables that best predict protection. The proposed research should provide an unbiased and comprehensive immune profiling approach to yield correlates of sterile protection that would guide development of next-generation malaria vaccines and help elucidate the mechanisms of PfSPZ-induced protection in infants living in a malaria-endemic area.
